# Cannonball

A multiplayer party battle royale built around the IUT ICT Fest 2026 GameJam theme, **Kickoff**. Every phase of the match is structured around the idea of a kickoff: the opening launch, the restart after every elimination, halftime, and a final sudden-kickoff duel.

## Concept

Players draft a loadout, get launched into an arena, and fight to keep a shared ball out of their own zone. Every time someone is eliminated, the match pauses for a "Restart Kickoff," and the player who just got knocked out gets to hand out an advantage and a curse before play resumes. Last player standing wins.

## Players

2 to 6, real time multiplayer.

## Core Structure

### 1. Kickoff (match start)

Before launch, each player drafts a loadout from three separate pools, picking one card from each:

- **Ability card.** An active thing your character can do (dash, shield, grapple hook).
- **Equipment card.** A passive item carried into the match (magnetic boots, a bumper shell, a speed anklet).
- **Gameplay Advantage card.** Changes how the round plays out in your favor (bigger starting zone, an early warning before a tick, one free save per round).

Each player ends up with exactly 3 cards: one ability, one equipment, one advantage. Cards have rarities (common, rare, epic); rarer cards are stronger and appear less often in the draft.

All players are then launched from cannons into the arena at the same time.

### 2. Arena Phase

The arena is a circle divided into wedge shaped zones, one per surviving player, with a ball roughly at the center. Moving platforms, wind, and obstacles keep the ball bouncing unpredictably so standing still is never fully safe. Players use their cards to push the ball toward opponents and away from their own wedge.

### 3. Tick (elimination check)

Every set number of seconds, the game checks which zone accumulated the most ball time during that interval, not just a single frozen instant at the exact tick. Whoever had the most ball time in their wedge since the last tick is eliminated. A visible danger meter per zone fills up in real time so everyone can see the risk building before it resolves.

This hybrid approach (interval accumulation, not a single snapshot) avoids the classic problem where only the last split second matters and the rest of the round feels irrelevant. It keeps tension high throughout the interval while still giving a clean dramatic beat at each tick.

### 4. Restart Kickoff (after every elimination)

This is the heart of the theme. When a player is eliminated, they don't just become a passive spectator. During the pause that follows:

- The ball resets to dead center.
- The eliminated player picks one surviving player to receive an **advantage card** and one to receive a **curse card** (can be the same player or different players).
- Both cards are auto generated by the game; the eliminated player only chooses who they go to.
- Effects are revealed publicly for spectacle, and expire at the next Restart Kickoff so they nudge the match rather than snowball it. This also naturally rubber-bands the leader, since eliminated players tend to curse whoever is winning.
- Play resumes with a short countdown.

This means a "kickoff" moment happens repeatedly throughout the match, not just once at the beginning, matching how a real kickoff restarts play after every stoppage.

### 5. Halftime

Once half the players have been eliminated, the match pauses for a bigger Halftime Kickoff: the arena reconfigures (wedges reshuffle, obstacles swap), everyone gets a slightly larger card offer than a normal restart, and play resumes with a fresh countdown.

### 6. Sudden Kickoff (final two)

When only two players remain, the arena shrinks to a clean two-zone split for a finale with its own identity, distinct from just "the same round with fewer people."

## Escalation

As players are eliminated, zones get fewer and larger (easier to physically avoid the ball), but ticks speed up and obstacles get more aggressive, keeping pace rising instead of slowing down as the lobby thins out.

## Art Style

Voxel/cube-block style (Minecraft-cube proportions with Fall Guys-style energy and color), not photorealistic. Characters are simple stacked box shapes: a torso, head, and four limbs.

## Technical Approach (Three.js)

- Characters are built procedurally out of primitive box geometries, parented in a hierarchy (torso as root, head and limbs as child groups). This acts as a simple rig without needing an imported, pre-skinned model.
- Animations (idle, walk, jump, land) are written as code: rotating and scaling the limb groups over time using sine waves and easing, the same technique the real Minecraft uses for its rigid character animation. No traditional skeletal skinning is required, which fits the blocky art style naturally.
- Multiplayer should use a lightweight sync layer (for example Colyseus, PartyKit, or a realtime database) rather than custom netcode. Favor syncing discrete state (draft picks, zone/tick results, restart events) over continuous physics, since that is much more realistic to get working solo in a short jam window.
